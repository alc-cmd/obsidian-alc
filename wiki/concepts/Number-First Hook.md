# Number-First Hook

A content pattern in which a post opens with a specific, concrete number — a count, ratio, or score — before making any claim. The number functions as a visual pattern-interrupt in-feed and an implicit receipt: it promises the claim is backed by measured reality, not opinion.

## Form

> *"1 in 7 — that's how many public TradingView strategies actually make money…"*
>
> *"155k stars on GitHub — thousands of curated AI prompts, all free."*
>
> *"A 16 year old just open sourced a free alternative to Screen Studio — 5k+ stars already."*

Structure: `<number> <unit> — <payoff claim>`

## Why it works

1. **Pattern-interrupt.** Most feed content opens with adjectives ("amazing", "crazy", "this changes everything"). A raw number disrupts the pattern and reads as higher-signal.
2. **Implied receipts.** The specificity ("1 in 7", not "most") signals actual measurement happened. Vague adjective hooks trigger skepticism; specific numbers bypass it.
3. **Compression.** The first 5–10 words carry the entire value proposition. Reader decides in one second whether to continue.
4. **Shareability.** The number becomes the memorable handle people use when recommending the post.

## Evidence

From [[X Analytics Q1-Q2 2026]] (90-day data on [[AlexAiSurfer]]'s account):

| Hook style | Example | Impr | ER |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Number-first | "1 in 7 TradingView strategies…" | 744 | 4.3% |
| Number-first | "155k stars on GitHub…" | 243 | 12.8% |
| Number-first | "5k+ stars on GitHub already" (Recordly) | 174 | 18.4% |
| Adjective-first | "Read slow, do your work…" | 320 | 3.8% |
| Abstract-first | "The taste one hits different…" | 117 | 12.8% |

Median ER on number-first originals is ~3× the account's weakest adjective-first originals. Caveat: number-first posts also tend to be tool-discovery posts, so the hook correlates with a topic bias that likely amplifies the effect.

## Application

- Lead every non-reactive original with a number. If you don't have one, the post isn't ready.
- Prefer ratios over raw counts when the ratio is more surprising ("1 in 7" > "236 strategies").
- Keep the number in the first five words — scrollers don't read past that.

## Anti-patterns

- Vanity numbers with no denominator ("10 productivity tips!") — reads as listicle spam.
- Numbers buried in paragraph two — wastes the pattern-interrupt.
- Inflated numbers without a source the reader can verify — erodes trust on the second post.
