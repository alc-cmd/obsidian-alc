# The Growing Gap in Understanding AI Capability

[[Andrej Karpathy]] argues that public perception of AI is bifurcating into two groups that are talking past each other. One group judges AI by free/old models and viral failure videos; the other uses state-of-the-art agentic models (Codex, Claude Code) professionally and is experiencing "AI Psychosis" from the staggering recent improvements.

## Key Takeaways
- There is a growing gap in public understanding of AI capability, driven by two factors: recency of use and tier of use
- Free/deprecated models don't reflect frontier agentic capabilities — but most people's views were formed by those models
- AI improvements are "peaky" — concentrated in technical domains (coding, math, research) rather than general queries (search, writing, advice)
- Two reasons capabilities are peaky:
  1. **Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards** — coding has clear success criteria (unit tests pass/fail), writing doesn't
  2. **B2B value** — technical domains generate more revenue, so companies focus R&D there
- The degree to which someone is awed by AI is perfectly correlated with how much they use AI to code
- The "OpenClaw moment" was significant because it was the first time non-technical people experienced agentic models beyond ChatGPT-as-a-website

## Summary

Karpathy identifies a perception split. Group 1 tried free ChatGPT, saw funny failures, and concluded AI is overhyped. Group 2 pays $200/month for frontier models, uses them for programming and research, and is experiencing capabilities that melt days/weeks of work into hours. These groups are speaking past each other. The gap exists because AI progress is unevenly distributed — concentrated in domains with verifiable reward signals and high B2B value.

A commenter cites OpenAI's own data: coding queries are only ~4% of ChatGPT messages while non-work queries make up 73%. For non-coding use cases, even paying subscribers have experienced stagnation. This validates Karpathy's point about the "peaky" nature of improvements.

## Notable Claims
- Frontier agentic models (Codex, Claude Code) can "go off for 1 hour to coherently restructure an entire code base, or find and exploit vulnerabilities in computer systems"
- This capability gap explains why AI discourse is so polarized — both sides are empirically correct about different aspects of the technology
- Companies are not sufficiently prioritizing non-technical use cases because they don't generate as much revenue
- Reinforcement learning's reliance on verifiable rewards is a structural reason why coding improves faster than writing or reasoning
